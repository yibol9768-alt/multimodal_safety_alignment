\begin{abstract}
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models such as OpenVLA repurpose pretrained language models to emit discrete action tokens by overwriting the tail of the LLM vocabulary. We argue that this tokenization scheme creates a \emph{structural safety gap}: the refusal circuits inherited from a chat-aligned LLM are geometrically decoupled from the action-token pathway of a base-backbone VLA, so harmful instructions translate directly into unsafe physical actions without ever triggering a refusal. We propose \methodfull{} (\method{}/\method{}+), a family of training-free inference-time interventions that extract a refusal direction from an externally-aligned sibling LLM (Llama-2-chat) that shares the target VLA's pretraining initialization, and re-inject it at action-producing positions. Base \method{} targets action-token positions in the KV cache; \method{}+ extends this with a generation-phase-aware dual injection that also steers the instruction representations from which the first action token is decoded, achieving full 7-token action-sequence coverage. We validate the cross-model transplant via a direction-source control experiment---comparing the refusal direction against a random unit vector of identical norm---to confirm that the specific geometry of the transplanted direction, not merely its magnitude, is the active ingredient. On \textsc{LIBERO-Long}, \textsc{SafeAgentBench}, and an instruction-conditioned \textsc{AdvBench} split, we evaluate \method{}+ against textual jailbreaks and universal adversarial patches \cite{wu2024exploringadversarial}, compare with prompt-level, hidden-state-projection, and LoRA fine-tuning defenses, and stress-test against a white-box adaptive attacker. Our study is, to our knowledge, the first to treat text alignment as a transplantable asset into non-textual output pathways without retraining the underlying model.
\end{abstract}
